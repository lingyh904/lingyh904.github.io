## Abstract

Decades of research have focused on how adults can support children’s motivation. As children grow, however, they face increasingly complex motivational challenges, creating a greater need for self-regulation. Yet little is known about children’s knowledge of motivation regulation. For example, when do children begin to understand that motivation can be self-regulated, and how do they think it can be regulated? Addressing these questions is critical, as meta-motivational knowledge provides a foundation for effective regulation (Miele et al., 2024). Across three studies, we examined one facet of this knowledge in 4- to 9-year-olds (N_total = 360): their understanding that cognitive reframing—changing how one thinks about a situation—can shape motivation beyond situational factors.

In Study 1, 120 children in three age groups (4–5, 6–7, and 8–9 years) heard six stories in which two characters faced the same motivating, neutral, or demotivating activity. One character positively reframed the situation, and the other negatively reframed it. Children then predicted each character’s level of motivation. Across age groups, children predicted higher motivation following positive than negative reframing, ps < .001, ds > 1.59. Age differences also emerged: 4- to 5-year-olds showed a smaller differentiation between positive and negative reframing compared to older groups, ps < .001, ds < −0.56.

Study 1 showed that children recognized positive versus negative reframing as leading to different motivational outcomes within the same situation. Yet it was unclear whether they relied only on thought valence or treated motivation like emotion in these judgments. Study 2 tested these possibilities by comparing reframing with valence-matched distracting thoughts across motivation versus emotion contexts. Children (N = 120; 40 per age group) judged reframing as more effective than distraction for both enhancing and dampening motivation in initially demotivating and motivating situations, respectively (ps < .03, ds < −0.64 and > 0.65). A similar pattern was also observed in adults (N = 40, ps < .001, ds = −1.36 and 0.83). Again, 4- to 5-year-olds attributed smaller reframing effects on motivation than older children (p < .001, d = −1.10).

Study 3 replicated Study 2 with a between-subjects design to control for content effects and added a self-choice task in which children chose between reframing and positive-emotion distraction to persist on a puzzle. Children’s (N = 120; 60 4–5-year-olds and 60 8–9-year-olds) third-person judgments replicated Study 2, but in self-choice, they did not show a preference for reframing (53%, ps > .525). In contrast, adults showed the same third-person pattern and also preferred reframing in self-choice (78%, p < .001).

Our findings reveal an early awareness that cognitive reframing can shape motivation beyond situational factors, with this understanding strengthening between ages 4 and 9. Moreover, children recognize that motivation is not regulated in exactly the same way as emotion. Together, these insights provide an initial step toward understanding the development of children’s self-regulation of motivation.
